Question 22 (3 marks): Hand washing with soap reduces the spread of diseases. Explain how soap helps remove pathogens from hands and why this is more effective than water alone.
Answer: Soap molecules have hydrophobic (water-repelling) and hydrophilic (water-attracting) ends. The hydrophobic end binds to oils and pathogens on skin, while hydrophilic end binds to water, allowing pathogens to be washed away. Water alone cannot remove oily residues containing pathogens.

Question 25 (4 marks): A hospital uses different materials to protect workers from radiation. Explain why paper suits protect against alpha radiation but lead aprons are needed for gamma radiation.
Answer: Alpha particles are large (2 protons + 2 neutrons), heavy, and charged. They interact strongly with matter and are stopped by paper/clothing. Gamma rays are electromagnetic waves with no mass/charge, so they penetrate deeply and require dense materials like lead to absorb them.

Question 27 (5 marks): Dalton believed atoms were solid, indivisible spheres. Explain how Thomson's cathode ray tube experiment challenged this model. What did Thomson discover and how did this change our understanding of atomic structure?
Answer: Thomson's cathode ray tube produced a beam that was attracted to positive plates, showing particles were negatively charged. He discovered electrons - small negative particles. This proved atoms are NOT indivisible solid spheres as Dalton thought. Thomson proposed atoms contain negative electrons embedded in positive material (plum pudding model), showing atoms have internal structure.

Question 30 (10 marks): A person receives a flu vaccine in October.
(a) Describe what the vaccine contains and how the body initially responds to it. (3 marks)

(b) Explain why this person is less likely to get flu in December, even though the vaccine was given two months earlier. (4 marks)

(c) Some people still get flu after vaccination. Suggest two reasons why vaccines are not 100% effective. (3 marks)
Answer (a): Vaccine contains dead/weakened flu virus or viral proteins (antigens). White blood cells recognize antigens as foreign and produce specific antibodies against them. Memory cells are created. (b): Memory cells remain in body for months/years. If real flu virus enters in December, memory cells quickly recognize it and rapidly produce large amounts of antibodies. Quick response kills virus before person becomes ill. (c): Flu virus mutates/changes antigens, so antibodies don't recognize new strain. Person's immune system may be weakened. Vaccine might not work as well in elderly/immunocompromised people. Multiple flu strains exist, vaccine only protects against predicted strains.
