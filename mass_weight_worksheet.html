Section 5: Short Answer Questions (6 points, 3 each)
Write your answers in complete sentences.
1. Mass vs Weight: Explain the difference between mass and weight using an example.
Mass is the amount of matter in an object and never changes. Weight is the force of gravity acting on that mass and changes with different gravity. For example, a 60 kg astronaut has the same mass on Earth and Moon, but weighs 588 N on Earth and only 96 N on the Moon.
2. Space Travel: Explain why understanding weight calculations is important for space missions.
Space missions need to know how much equipment will weigh on other planets for landing calculations, fuel requirements, and designing spacecraft. Astronauts also need to understand how different gravity will affect their movement and the operation of equipment on other worlds.

Scenario: A space mission is planning to send a 150 kg rover to Mars and then to Jupiter's moon Europa (g = 1.3 m/s²).
a) Calculate the rover's weight on Earth.

b) Calculate the rover's weight on Mars.
Click to show answer
W = m × g = 150 kg × 3.7 m/s² = 555 N
c) Calculate the rover's weight on Europa.
Click to show answer
W = m × g = 150 kg × 1.3 m/s² = 195 N
d) On which location would the rover be easiest to move around? Explain why.
Europa would be easiest because the rover weighs the least there (195 N). Lower weight means less force needed to move the rover around, making it easier to operate and requiring less energy for movement.
